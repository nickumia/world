I grew up in a household where the rules weren't philosophical; they were practical; a place where the air was always a mix of ambition, caution, and the quiet panic that comes from knowing how thin the line is between stability and collapse. No one hated nuance, but nuance didn't keep the lights on. The deficit of money was filled with discipline. There wasn't a roadmap for success, but there were expectations...loud ones. I learned quickly, in that environment, that I don't get to drift. I don't get to experiment with failure. I didn't get to "see what happens." The message was simple:

Work hard, stay out of trouble, don't fail.

So.. I adjusted. I became the kid who always had to make it work. School? Excel. Work? Go beyond. Any activity? Become the best by any means necessary (..as long as no one knew the whole story). There wasn't a conversation about it; it was simply the the safest place to stand. Love was always there, just in a way shaped by scarcity (side note: in my head, there are two competing understandings, of the love that I received and maybe I'll talk about that another time); love found its way through pressure, through warnings, through the relentless reminder that the world out there does not have patience for people who don't get it right the first time.

Not because failure was shameful; but because failure was dangerous.

Failure meant slipping backward. Our parents risked their life to get this opportunity. Failure meant risking this opportunity and disrespecting the sacrifices that made it possible. I couldn't do that. Yet, the more I lived.. through travel (my first time away from home in Rochester, NY), through mistakes (honestly, too many to count), through conversations with strangers (momentary friends in college and beyond), through long quiet nights alone with my own thoughts ..the more the puzzle pieces started rearranging themselves. And eventually, after carrying this question for most of my life, I reached a conclusion that surprised even me:

  • Good and bad aren't opposites.
  • They're mirrors.
  • Both of them orbit around self-interest.
  • Both of them are tools.
  • Both of them tell a story ... but not the truth.

And along the way, with personal success giving me freedom to take risks, I outgrew the very framework that raised me. That's when the strange part came.. when I realized I'd gone further than anyone expected.. further than anyone around me could help me understand. I learned things my parents never had access to. Life had taken me in directions they never had the luxury to explore. I found myself asking spiritual questions, diving headfirst into philosophies, experiences, and inner worlds that simply aren't part of the world I was raised in. Not out of rebellion.. not because I thought I was above anything.. but because I had set something in motion that I couldn't ignore. I needed to see what was underneath the surface everyone else seemed content to stand on.

This is how my mind ended up circling a question that started when I was barely old enough to understand it; a question that didn't fit neatly into the rulebook I had inherited:

Is anything truly good or truly bad? If yes, are there fundamentally good and/or fundamentally bad things?

Not in the "society says so" way. Not in the "my parents taught me" way. I mean.. at the root.. at the level where value exists.. beneath culture.. beneath convenience.. beneath fear. It was something that grew quietly over the years, in the cracks between the life I was handed and the life I was building. All the old moral structures I grew up with started to feel different. Not wrong. Just… misplaced. Too shallow for the depth I was actually trying to understand.

At some point I realized I had spent most of my life trying to be "good" without ever asking what that word actually meant and, by extension, who/what decided the meaning in the first place. This blog is me continuing that search; the pressures that shaped me, the questions that chased me, and the experiences that keep rewriting the way I understand the world.

...once I pulled on this thread, everything in my life started to unravel...

Four days ago, I finally found a clue about the deeper nature..

Good and bad are both selfish. Equally selfish. Not opposite, not balanced, just the same.

Good and Bad Aren't Just Labels, They're Self-Serving Labels

Here's what I mean.

People often think the "selfishness" of good and bad lives in the person doing the action:

  • "I helped someone because it made me feel good."
  • "I judged something as bad because it threatened my interests."

And yes, that's part of it. Individuals use moral labels to reinforce their own identity, protect their own values, and justify their own emotions.

But that's only one layer.

The labels themselves, the entire architecture of "good" and "bad", represent an inherent selfishness. This nature is used by society to frame stories in ways that maintain order, control behavior, justify norms, and simplify complex realities into digestible moral sound bites. They reward conformity and punish deviation, even when the deviation might be necessary or illuminating.

It doesn't matter whether you zoom into the individual or zoom out to society: the labeling system is self-serving at every level of abstraction.

And once you notice that the framework is flawed, you start to see that any argument built on top of it will also be flawed. Flawed, not in the sense of "wrong", but in the sense of not being the entire picture. By design, it focuses on parts of the whole.

There is an important nuance here. Light and Dark. Positive and Negative. Hot and Cold. There are natural opposites in life. There are two specific dual terms that we are talking about here which are:

  • Good and Bad
  • Right and Wrong

Action → Consequence. Everything after that is a projection.

Here's the clearest way I can describe the world as I'm starting to see it:

Every action leads to a consequence. That's it. Everything else is a narrative told by the observer.

Consequences aren't moral. They aren't emotional. They aren't good or bad. They're simply the natural byproduct of actions. They have attributes, e.g. harm, help, growth, loss, destruction, expansion, contraction, but these aren't moral qualities. They're just descriptions of how energy rearranges itself.

When we judge a consequence as good or bad, we aren't describing the consequence. We're describing our attachment to the outcome.

And attachment is inherently selfish. That doesn't mean it's evil; just self-oriented.

It's like trying to judge whether wind is "good" or "bad." The wind doesn't care. The wind just is. Your house getting destroyed or your kite flying higher is your experience of the wind, not the wind itself. (There are times when talking about the wind as "good" or "bad" is useful. And we'll revisit this idea later.)

A consequence simply exists. The moment we moralize it, we distort it.

War, Perspective, and the Collapse of Justification

Take war. Each side thinks, "We're the good guys."

They don't start with a neutral weighing of ethics and then conclude, "Yes, this war is justified." It works in reverse: their identity, fears, history, and worldview create the conclusion first. "Good" is then attached afterward as the explanation.

In that sense, goodness becomes self-serving. "Good" becomes the armor wrapped around whatever the group already believed.

Arguing about who is "right" in a war is usually a waste of energy. Each side is fighting from inside its own story, and those stories actively deny the existence of the other side. In that world, "good" and "bad" don't illuminate anything. Perspectives become justification. There's no clean separation between "how I see the world" and "why I think I'm right." Justification is just perspective dressed up in narrative, made to feel "moral" and inevitable.

Why We Should Be More Conscious of Using Good/Bad

I'm not saying we should throw these words away entirely. I'm saying we should understand what they actually do.

Good and bad:

  • "help us" compress real-life complex systems into cartoon-like stories, converting overwhelming complexity into something we can act on,
  • "distract us" from problem-solving,
  • give us quick shorthand for our values and preferences,
  • "make us" focus on who "wins" the narrative, not what actually helps, which shifts our attention from "what's actually happening" to "which interpretation wins,"
  • invite outside spectators to judge or interfere even when they're far from the situation,
  • "give us an excuse" to stop thinking once we've chosen a side, making us feel thinking is complete once we've labeled something.

When we "moralize" a situation, we stop being curious about its actual mechanics. We stop asking:

  • What actually happened?
  • What is the consequence?
  • What do the involved parties actually need?
  • What solution genuinely works here?

Instead, we argue over abstractions.

We argue over team colors.

Good/bad becomes a substitute for thinking; and eventually, a substitute for understanding.

Justice Is a Victim of the Good/Bad Illusion

This is where justice becomes tricky.

Most justice systems are built on the belief that good must triumph over bad. That sounds noble, until you realize that the categories themselves are unstable.

If good and bad are flawed frames, then any justice built on them is inherently biased. It doesn't matter if you optimize for outcomes, or process, or fairness; you're still starting from a broken foundation.

And I don't have the answer yet. I'm working on it. I don't know what a justice system without moral categories looks like. But I do know this:

Justice will never be complete until we fix its underlying concept of "good vs. bad."

That part I'm sure of.

Where This Leaves Me ...For Now

I had the realization that "good and bad are equally selfish" only four days ago. It's fresh. It's alive. It's still shifting in me. I'm not done exploring it.

But the more I sit with it, the more I notice how it makes room for clarity. I'm not pretending moral categories are universal when they aren't. I'm not pretending consequences need my opinion to be real. I'm not trying to solve the puzzle of "good or bad" anymore. I'm trying to understand what is and what might be possible once we stop using broken tools to measure reality.

This is an ongoing story. I'm living it as I write this. And I'm hoping that, by naming it out loud, I'll keep uncovering whatever comes next.

Why do we call things good or bad? And who benefits from those labels?
