The Theory

In this blog, I will attempt to convey this theory. You will attempt to refute it.

The theory, if you will allow me, is about choosing where to anchor your attention, i.e. be present now or plan for the future. It rests on two main ideas that define how we experience time:

  1. The Hidden Architecture of Identity (The Glimpses or micro-moments): True self-definition is housed in intense, brief micro-moments that offer a glimpse into a depth of life beyond the ordinary, planned illusion. We spend our lives chasing the infinite; but over the course of a day, micro-seconds matter; and over the course of a human life, a grand total of minutes matter; and the infinite is often captured in these glimpses if we are able to see them.
  2. The Necessary Anchor (The Awareness): A fulfilling journey requires actively cultivating the awareness to recognize these defining moments. This means mastering the tension between mapping the future (Planner) and surrendering to the now (Presence) and consciously choosing where to set the anchor of our attention. It is in this conscious choice that we find the true essence of living.

I was in the pool this evening, as the sun began to set; the sun glistening gold across the water. The water's natural beauty was in full display; a cleansing feeling that washes away attachment and frees the soul...

It's an outdoor pool, and the air had that perfect, suspended quality; a breeze that wasn't cold, just cool enough to create a sharp contrast against the warmth of the exertion.

Swimming is usually my off-switch. It's the best form of exercise to clear my mind and rebalance my body. Most of the time, I'm just on auto-pilot in the water: breath, stroke, turn, repeat. I'll focus on a mantra, or just count the laps, or zone in on the specific burn in a muscle group and, without realizing it, everything else fades away.

But there are these tiny gaps. Usually, right when I hit the wall, I pause for a moment. In that pause, the brain reorients itself and I see the world in a very unique way. It was in that moment that an image, an idea, a feeling emerged.. navigating those floating rocks.

I started imagining myself stepping through a series of floating rocks in mid-air. They are static. Or at least they seem to be. It's hard to tell in constant motion. But they seem to go on endlessly. There was no beginning of the idea, nor any meaningful end in sight. It was just a continuous motion of walking on these rocks, where the sole goal is to not fall or miss one.

We're walking on one now, and we have to move forward. The tension. The real struggle. It's where we put our eyes.

There is a seduction to planning. We want to map out the next five, ten, twenty rocks. We want to engineer the path to the future. But the energy required to calculate the trajectory of the tenth rock while you're balancing on the current one is exhausting. And often, by the time you get to the next rock, the path has shifted anyway. The plan was an illusion of control.

So, the alternative is to narrow the aperture. You focus entirely on the single stone right in front of you. You commit to the now. This increases your accuracy for that one step, sure. You're less likely to slip today. But does it make the journey reliable? Or does it just mean you're moving confidently toward an obstacle you didn't see coming because you were looking at your feet?

In fact, the act of looking ahead is vital. It's exactly like driving a car: if you only focus on the asphalt a few feet in front of your bumper, your hands will get jumpy, the car will swerve, and you'll make constant, small mistakes. Focusing further down the road (not ten miles ahead), but far enough to sense the curve, smooths out the motion. It doesn't guarantee safety, but it makes the journey steady. Focus too far ahead and you'll miss potholes or a person running across the road or some other unexpected obstacle.

All this to say.. there isn't a "right" answer here. I think the "choice" between planning and presence is one of many tug-of-wars that shape our experience. It's not about solving it; it's about noticing the tension.

The Windows We Usually Miss

This duality hit me hard as I kept swimming, moving through the water as this idea was expanding in my mind. I never try to orchestrate the workout. I just let myself flow. The thought that came to me completely changed the texture of the experience that I was already having.

I started noticing the glimpses.

We usually talk about life in chapters (or seasons).. college.. marriage.. the new job.. the move.. retirement.. But life doesn't actually happen in chapters. It happens in micro-seconds. It happens in flashes that are so intense and fleeting that if you blink, or if you're too busy looking at the "tenth rock," you miss them entirely (only to realize it later in life; what was right there all along).

I experienced these micro-seconds profoundly during my SpiritualTech road trip. The mountains of New Mexico and the stark deserts of Texas and Utah offered a silence that forced introspection; the kind of space where your mind stops rushing and just *sees*. Contrast that with the humid, overwhelming green of Kentucky, or the eerie stillness of various deserted towns scattered across the nation. I saw these glimpses in the ceremonial air of capitol buildings, in the unique downtown architecture, in hikes to remote areas, and in the quiet, enduring life of small towns and Native American tribal lands. It's the difference between planning the map and realizing the map is actually drawn by these unexpected moments.

I'm talking about the way the light refracted through the water for a split second, creating a pattern in the pool water that looked surreal. Or the sensation of the air hitting your lungs after a long underwater glide. Super fleeting experiences in the way the world exists in this moment and only in that moment.

These moments aren't just filler scenes between the big events. They are the events. They are the windows into the deeper life we're usually too distracted to recognize. They create the life we spend our whole lives trying to understand.

It's strange, isn't it? We spend so much energy trying to stabilize the ground beneath us, trying to predict the future, that we forget that the magic is usually happening in the unstable, unscripted air between the steps.

We're so focused on the destination that we miss the view from the journey. I don't have the balance figured out yet. I still catch myself trying to map all of the rocks aheads of me. But tonight, at least, I'm letting myself just be in the moment and appreciate the water while I'm in it.

How about you? When was the last time you felt yourself pause and really see one of those glimpses?
