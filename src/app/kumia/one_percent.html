I've been thinking about the idea of "the 1%," and I don't really like it because it's one of those phrases that's loaded before you even touch it. Most people hear it and immediately picture the same thing: ultra-wealthy people, excess, some vague moral failure wrapped in money.

But that version of the 1% feels... incomplete.

There are so many other 1%s that don't get as much attention. The percentage of people who run a marathon. Or who reach a certain depth underwater. Or who learn how to fly a plane. Or who leave the planet entirely. Even things that sound almost boring on paper, like the number of people who manage to retire in their early forties. It's not like the 'money 1%' that pervades most of life. When I read that statistic recently about the early retirees and saw it was less than 1%, I paused for a real minute. Not because it felt impressive, but because I'd never framed it that way before.

It made me wonder how many of the things we chase aren't really chosen.. We just learn about them and seek to replicate the "success" we see.

I've been pursuing financial independence for a long time now, more intentionally in the last few years. I'm just about wrapping up my late twenties, and depending on how things unfold, I could reach it in as little as three more years, or maybe closer to eight. There's a bit of quiet disbelief ... It's not like I've solved some magic growth formula. There is nothing guaranteed about my financial future. But it's definitely very surreal to watch the last six months produce the same amount what once took me FIVE years to accrue.

And something's been bothering me... not the goal itself, but the way goals get handed down to us. No one really taught me what I know about finance.. There were soo many experiences that got me to that point. If I had followed any individual experience, I would be in a very different place... We're taught what "winning" looks like long before we understand ourselves well enough to decide whether that version of winning even fits. Somewhere along the way, copying someone else's path becomes easier than sitting with the discomfort of asking what we actually want.

There's nothing wrong with copying. It's how humans learn. But copying only works if the person you're copying resonates with you on a deeper level. It's not just in the outcome, but in the lifestyle, tradeoffs, and values. Otherwise, you end up chasing a life that looks right from the outside and feels alien from the inside.

Potential doesn't show up the same way for everyone because people have different natural orientations. Certain directions feel stabilizing; others feel destabilizing even when things are "going well." Progress in the wrong direction still moves you forward, just away from yourself. Some paths return energy as you move through them. Others only take.

When success is defined in a way that continually drains you...

When you are forced into the wrong version of "success"...

Discipline stops producing growth and starts breaking connection.

I don't have a clean takeaway here. I'm still in the middle of it. But the question that keeps coming back is simple, and uncomfortable, and probably worth sitting with longer than we usually do:

What 1% are you actually trying to belong to?

Not the one that sounds impressive. Not the one you inherited. The one that would still matter if no one was watching.

It's a very personal question. But the answer is very real. If you are able to answer it, people who truly know you will be able to see you live the answer. I think asking the question might be the beginning of something more honest.
