The End at the Beginning
I wanted to say, "we like to accumulate". What I ended up getting to was that "accumulation allows us to exist". I would dial back my ending statement to "if an accumulation engine exists, humans exist." However, I don't think we're a special case in this scenario.

When this idea first came to me, I thought that accumulation was a human quality. After sitting with the idea, accumulation is more of a symptom of something deeper. Accumulation isn't what we think. At first glance, one might say we like to collect "things" ... experiences, material things, relationships, knowledge. It's easy to want to stop there. Humans just like to "see the numbers go up". Borrowing from my other post about quality and quantity, we want.. more, even when "more" is subjective; more quality or more quantity. And even building on my post about trying to be a 1%-er, we want.. more, even when having "more" means being less, by virtue of being more unique.

But something's wrong...

Accumulation isn't just something people do. It's something people lean on to prove that things about them are actually carrying forward. Everyone accumulates. But not everything that accumulates actually becomes part of you.

There are two interesting concepts I'll note here: (1) the Vedic Lifecycle which is about accumulating knowledge to prepare you to give it up once you are done with it and (2) the power of Transient AI which uses accumulated knowledge to "do something" without actually accumulating anything.

In the truest sense of the reference, we are born with nothing "physical" and leave with nothing "physical". What happens to intangibles like knowledge, experiences, and relationships? Well, for argument sake, those are actually physical. Even if you accumulate knowledge within a lifetime, you don't know you know what-you-know when you die. So it's limited by being manifested in this reality. Similar arguments apply to experiences and relationships.

The current state of AI, I'll argue, doesn't accrue because it is literally a point-in-time human. It doesn't actually have a feedback loop where it "reflects" on an event/experience and then modify behaviors like humans. It doesn't build an "ego"/self/identity. However, it possesses a perspective (remniscent of an identity) when it performs a task. Whatever growth it may display is borrowed accumulation from the varied inputs and changes to its parameters.

Coming back to the point, when things go wrong ... not everything you acquire accumulates. We may never see the value of some experiences. We may learn things we aren't able to apply. We may build relationships that don't last. Even if we do acquire something, we may realize that it doesn't actually build into something that we value (and it becomes more like debt than value).

Because we don't know/fully-understand this concept of value... From the outside, it all looks like growth (i.e. things gained). But from the inside, it may feel like drift. There's an assumption most people don't question: That time naturally compounds. That what you got yesterday meaningfully becomes who you are today. But that's not guaranteed. It's aspirational. Humans don't perfectly accumulate. They fragment. They forget. They contradict themselves. They reset in ways that erase more than they carry forward.

Accumulation becomes a symptom of our "self" attempting to persist. If our self is not reinforced, the "high" of accumulation doesn't hit.

So, in essence, even the people who say "I don't like change" or "I won't change" are seeking to accumulate to reinforce that point. For the people who are open to change or have goals in mind, they are accumulating in an effort to validate that point. And we don't like accumulation if it isn't continuous; continuous from one idea to the same idea or from one idea to a new idea.

If we remove the "accumulation engine", the sense of self starts to flicker. Life begins to feel like disconnected moments instead of a trajectory. I think the reason people don't get to see this point is because they adopted some system or framework that has an embedded accumulation engine. Their entire mindset and behaviors are driving accumulation in a predetermined way that removes the need to question or understand the mechanism.

As AI is breaking down the old systems and frameworks, people are realizing that they don't understand the mechanisms behind what they're doing and more of these invisible pieces will start to appear. Revisiting the point about "leaving with nothing", accumulation doesn't actually belong to individuals in a clean way. And tying that to the power of "ego-less AI", AI has almost perfected the engine. In the smallest units of transaction, use accumulation to do something and then give it up. It's important to say that AI is as state-less as humans. Neither are stateless. We both have a context that we operate in; however, AI doesn't attach to the state like humans.

(I might argue that a future AI system that has a feedback loop is inferior to the current implementation. It would make it more human which might not be a "good" thing.)

In an attempt to say the same point differently... Accumulation flows through system. Knowledge accumulates in "libraries" or "databases". Experience accumulates in cultures. Energy accumulates in storage mediums (nature, systems, ...). Identity accumulates in relationships/connections. You don't create accumulation. You inherit accumulation. You interact with it. If you're intentional, you can hold onto a piece of it long enough for it to influence you.

Understanding that accumulation is less about the "what" and more defined as the "how", might be just be the point. And value might be the unit of transaction, which is why it's so elusive. It's not about quantity/quality. It's about persistence.

Value is constantly being transformed. Most of it passes through the accumulation engine. Value becomes the essence of self that we presume is what we are accumulating. The key is just that it is transformed and not necessarily only gained. It's not something we can chase. It's not something we can optimize. The "growth" we witness is more about the magic of the engine than anything we can control.

I guess... there are limits to just using the same engine over and over again. There are drawbacks to changing engines too frequently. There are downsides to using incompatible engine types back to back... So finding that "golden" engine sequence allows you to achieve a desire you set. The notion of "life" might be that an accumulation engine exists at all. In other words...

if an accumulation engine exists, life exists.
