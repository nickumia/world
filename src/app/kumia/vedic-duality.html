The Foundational Binary

Unmanifest & Manifest

In the Vedic tradition, the distinction between Light and Darkness is not a moral opposition: it is the primary ontological duality underlying all of reality. This begins with a fundamental binary: the Unmanifest (Avyākṛta / Asat) and the Manifest (Vyākṛta / Sat). These are not sequential stages but co-existing modes of the same underlying Awareness (Chit). What we perceive as light is the manifest world coming into being; what we call darkness is the infinite, unmanifest potential from which it ceaselessly emerges.

🌑
Darkness / Unmanifest
Avyākṛta

The undifferentiated womb. Not emptiness, but Fullness (Pūrṇa): every possibility held simultaneously. The Seed State before name or form (Nāma-Rūpa) has arisen. Silent. Infinite. Complete. Everything is already present, so nothing needs to be sought — it is silent.

The Answer with no Question.

🔥
Light / Manifest
Vyākṛta

The brought-forth world. Existence, order, and the structured laws of Ṛta (cosmic order). When Agni is kindled, the world is made visible. Patterned. Named. Real. The moment the One becomes Many, a gap is created; and that gap is the question. Life is the pursuit of returning to the Answer.

The Question with no Answer.

Nāsadīya Sūkta · Rig Veda 10.129

The hymn begins by stripping away every binary we use to understand the manifest world: Neither Existence nor Non-Existence ... No Space, No Sky ... No Death, No Immortality ... The One Breathed, Windless.

"Darkness there was at first,
by darkness hidden —
Undifferentiated depth was this all."

The Creation Hymn · The Definitive Statement of the Unmanifest

This hymn does not describe a past event. It gestures toward a condition that is ever-present beneath the surface of the manifest world; the ground state of pure potential that never ceased to exist, even as the cosmos poured forth from it.

An object "exists" (is manifest) only when it is illumined by awareness. When the light of attention moves away, that entity recedes back into pure potential. It loses its name and form and becomes the Seed once more.

Advaita Vedānta · Kashmiri Śaivism
Nāsadīya Sūkta · Rig Veda 10.129.4-5

"In the beginning, there was Desire, which was the first seed of mind;
sages having meditated in their hearts have discovered by their wisdom —
the connection of the existent with the non-existent."

The Creation Hymn · The Transition from the Unmanifest

Nāsadīya Sūkta · Rig Veda 10.129.6-7

"Who really knows?
Who will here proclaim it?
Whence was it produced?
Whence is this creation?"

The Creation Hymn · The Unknown Origin of the Manifest

The Rishis suggest that even the Gods came after the manifest world was created, so even they might not know the true origin. It posits that perhaps the "Overseer in the highest heaven" knows... or "perhaps he does not know."

The Two Faces of Desire

Kāma & Trishnā

English collapses a critical Sanskrit distinction into a single word: desire. The Vedic tradition draws a sharp line between two entirely different forces:

Primordial Will · The Fuel
Kāma

The "Aesthetic Push": the non-negotiable pressure of life to express itself. Why a flower blooms. Why you build a system. It is Ontological: about Being, not Getting. It is the Light hitting the Seed.

Even an enlightened being (Jīvanmukta) eats, speaks, and teaches; this is Kāma still operating.

Psychological Craving · The Hook
Trishnā / Rāga

The "Thirst": the mental parasite that attaches to the impulse. It says: "I am incomplete unless X happens." It is Egoic: about Getting, not Being.

This is what must reach Zero for enlightenment. Not Kāma.

Enlightenment doesn't drain the tank; it unfastens the hook. You can move at 100 mph through the manifest world and still be at "Zero Desire" if your internal peace isn't tethered to the outcome.

Desire as Architect of Reality

The Kāma-Vidyā (Science of Desire) in the Upaniṣads teaches that the universe isn't just made by a primordial desire at the beginning of time; it is being reconstructed by your desire in every micro-second.

Attention as Gravity

Whatever you value (the root of desire) acts as gravity for your attention. Attention then organizes your reality.

Crystallized Desire

Your current life is a physical, crystallized form of a prior desire. You are not a victim of circumstances; you are the downstream result of a Sankalpa.

Vāsanās
वासना

Latent "scents" or tendencies left over from previous desires. The invisible current beneath the water; you don't see them, but they dictate where the boat goes.

Vāsanā-Daha
वासना दह

"The Burning of Tendencies." We may still be living inside a manifestation whose inner Sankalpa has already moved on; creating a "push" from an old background process.

Identifying the Push

The question should not be "What should I do?"

The question should be "What desire led me to where I am, and am I still in alignment with it?"

When you feel the push, ask:


  • Is this Kāma: a pull toward new creative form, a natural unfolding of the Light?
  • Or Trishnā: a push away from a perceived lack, a craving tethered to a specific outcome?

If it is Trishnā, a Sankalpa planted from that state will feel like a burden. If it is Kāma, it will feel like the natural next emergence.

The Alchemy of Change

Dharma & Sankalpa

Once you are manifest (already here, already in motion) you must operate within the "physics" of your current form: this is Dharma. And yet the seed of the next form is already germinating: this is where Sankalpa enters.

Dharma serving Structural Integrity

Dharma is not a fixed "duty" or a sentence to serve. It is the Structural Weight; the momentum of your current manifestation. Because you have a particular shape in the world (a profession, family ties, relationships, reputation), you have a functional form. A bridge's Dharma is to hold weight. It doesn't desire to do so; it simply is that function.

Responsibility as Weight

Dharma feels like gravity; the inertia of the manifest. "Because I am X, I must do Y." It is stationary power.

The Conflict Signal

We feel "burdened" when our current Dharma (the shape we've taken) no longer matches our current Kāma (the direction we want to expand). This friction is diagnostic, not fatal.

Sankalpa creating The Formed Intention

A Sankalpa is not a New Year's resolution. It is a seed planted in the Unmanifest, at the level of Bliss/Intention, before the intellect, mind, or body have processed it. This is why it works differently from willpower-based planning. Using a Sankalpa isn't avoiding work; it is optimizing the direction of work. You are acknowledging that your current Dharma was itself created by an old Sankalpa. Planting a new one simply begins the next harvest.

You are what your deep, driving desire is. As your desire is, so is your will. As your will is, so is your deed. As your deed is, so is your destiny.

— Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 4.4.5

This quote reveals the chain of downstream causation: Destiny (current Dharma) is just the final echo of an old desire. Change the desire at the top, and the destiny at the bottom must eventually follow.

Is Using a Sankalpa "Cheating"?

This question arises from a modern misunderstanding that "hard work" (manifest-level action) is the only legitimate way to change circumstances. The Vedas offer two modes:

Method

Description

Vedic Term
Hardwork Brute force: manifest-level planning and action. Moving the mountain shovel by shovel. Rājasic Action
Changing Gravity Fluid: shifting the underlying desire so the mountain naturally realigns over time. Work continues, but in a new direction. Sāttvic Sankalpa

The Purushārthas — Dharma and Kāma as Partners

In the Vedic "Four Goals of Life" (Purushārthas), Dharma and Kāma are not enemies — they are different layers of the manifest state, meant to work in concert:

Zero Trishnā, Not Zero Kāma

Enlightenment

"Zero Desire" as a description of enlightenment is accurate; but only when we understand which desire reaches zero. If zero desire meant the total extinction of all impulse, you would stop breathing. The heart would stop. You would physically unmanifest. That is technicaly the ultimate form. However, to be enlightened while manifest, you need to maintain Kāma (Primordial Will) while eliminating Trishnā (Craving/Attachment).

The Precise Target

Enlightenment = Zero Trishnā (Craving/Attachment)while Kāma (Primordial Will) continues to operate through you freely. You become a Clear Lens. Light still moves through, but there is no distortion from the ego's hooks. Notice: "acting without longing." Not sitting motionless. Not refusing to engage. The enlightened being acts. Perhaps even at high intensity. But without the internal hook that says "I am incomplete unless this particular outcome arrives."

A person who has abandoned all desires and is acting without longing, free from the sense of "I" and "mine" — he attains peace.

— Bhagavad Gītā 2.71

Enlightenment and Happiness — Ānanda, Not Pleasure

The equation for enlightenment being interpreted as "happiness" is accurate only if we use the Sanskrit term Ānanda — which means "Non-dual Bliss," not emotional pleasure.

Type

Nature

Stability
Standard Happiness Depends on a manifest object: "I am happy because X happened." Since objects change, happiness is temporary. Conditional · Impermanent
Ānanda The joy of being the Source itself — the Unmanifest recognizing its own nature. Does not depend on what the Light is currently hitting. Unconditional · Permanent

You don't attain Ānanda. You are Ānanda. Desires (cravings) are clouds that temporarily block the sun. When Trishnā reaches zero, the clouds dissolve and your natural state (Bliss) is all that remains.

Two Paths Through the Manifest World

The Unaligned Path

Plant a seed out of lack: "I need this to be happy." Spend years in anxiety waiting for it to manifest. The outcome defines your peace.

The Enlightened Path

Plant a seed out of play: "It would be beautiful to manifest this." Spend years in Presence, fully operating in current Dharma, already Full — regardless of whether the seed grows.

He who is everywhere without attachment, who neither rejoices nor is vexed — his wisdom is fixed.

— Bhagavad Gītā 2.57

Balance, Ascent, or Transcendence?

The Three Guṇas

The Guṇas are the three fundamental qualities (threads) of Prakṛti; the manifest world. As long as you have a body and operate in time, all three are present. The confusion is whether they should be balanced, ascended through, or transcended entirely. The common teaching of "aim for Sattva only" is a trap. It produces what might be called Spiritual Tamas — passivity, avoidance, and the loss of the generative push. The answer is more nuanced...

Tamas
तमस्

Darkness · Inertia · Rest. Heaviness, stability, the tendency toward stasis.

You need this to sleep, to have stable bones, and to stop moving. The Landing Gear.

Rajas
रजस्

Heat · Passion · Movement. The drive to act, achieve, and change form.

You need this to eat, walk, integrate, build, and execute. The Engines.

Sattva
सत्त्व

Light · Clarity · Harmony. Lucidity, the capacity to perceive truth.

You need this to understand, to feel Presence, to see clearly. The Pilot in the cockpit.

Stage

Relationship to Guṇas

Goal
Ordinary Life All three Guṇas operate unconsciously; Tamas and Rajas dominate alternately Develop more Sattva — clarity as the ground
Sādhaka (Seeker) Sattva is cultivated as the centering force; Rajas is directed, Tamas is managed Use Sattva to direct Rajas and time Tamas
Guṇātīta (Liberated) The observer stands outside the triangle and watches all three Guṇas play without being pulled Transcendence — neither attached to Sattva nor averse to Tamas
The Correct Frame

You don't aim for "Sattva only." You aim to center yourself in Sattva so you can direct Rajas (execution) and tame Tamas (rest). The ultimate goal, Guṇātīta, is not super-Sattvic living but standing outside the triangle entirely, watching the Guṇas operate without being governed by any of them. A truly Guṇātīta day is neither better when Sāttvic nor worse when Tāmasic. The observer is stable regardless of which Guṇa is currently dominant.

The Guṇas — Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas — born of Prakṛti, bind the embodied Self in the body. Of these, Sattva, being pure, causes illumination and health; it binds by attachment to happiness and knowledge.

— Bhagavad Gītā 14.5–6 (condensed)

Note the subtlety: even Sattva binds — through attachment to clarity and happiness. The Guṇātīta state is beyond even this attachment. Sattva is the ladder, not the roof.

Living Application

Presence as Connection to the Source

This duality maps directly onto the inner life. The manifest and unmanifest are not just cosmic categories; they describe two modes of consciousness available in every moment. There are those attempting to plan out life, being in the illusion of control; and, there are those who are present in the moment, accepting the natural flow of existence.

📋
Planning
Manifest · Ignorance

An attempt to project light into a future void. Requires form, boundary, and "seen" outcomes. It structures the unmanifest into anticipated shape: the mind casting Prakāśa forward.

🌊
Presence
Unmanifest · Awareness

A leaning back into the undifferentiated depth. Being comfortable with pure potential before it collapses into a specific "thing." Recognition that the only manifest is what the light is hitting right now.

The Unmanifest is not empty — it is Full, Pūrṇa. It is the state of holding every possible light-wave at once. This is why it appears as Darkness to our limited eyes: not absence, but excess beyond what form can contain.

Advaita Vedānta

The Sacred Symbol

Agni — The Living Threshold

Seen through this lens, Agni, the Vedic fire, is not a campfire. He is Prakāśa embodied: the principle of illumination that continuously calls the manifest world into being. Every kindling of the ritual fire enacts the primordial emergence of form from formlessness.

When the fire goes out, the ritual does not fail: it returns to the Unmanifest. The seed state is restored. And in that darkness, everything waits, whole and complete, for the next kindling.
